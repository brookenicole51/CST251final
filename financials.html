For the MLB, baseball is more than just a sport. It is a multi-billion dollar business that relies on the fanâ€™s devotion and viewer participation in order to be successful. With COVID-19 hitting our country earlier this year, the MLB had to make major changes in order to continue the season without losing money. One of the first major changes was the shortening of the 2020 season. With only 60 games to play, the league lost tons of viewership and attendance money on the other 100 games that would have played out. Another major impact was the absence of fans, which was a huge impact since no tickets were being sold. Stadiums felt dead and empty, and players even commented on how weird it was to not have a cheering crowd. TV networks had little to no content to film besides the game itself, and the overall mood of the arenas was not the same. The financial impact of COVID-19 differs from team to team, however. Since most players are signed under a contract, many new rules and regulations were put in place to make sure players were paid fairly. Many contracts were changed, extended, and cut because of the virus, and it definitely affected every player financially. This year has been a challenge for many sports, but the MLB has been lucky enough to complete the 2020 season (somewhat) safely. Even though the financial side of the MLB took a massive hit, that does not change the fact that thousands of fans will hopefully return to the field next season to support their beloved baseball teams.
